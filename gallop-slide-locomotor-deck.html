Understanding the Skills

Gallop vs Slide — Same Pattern, Different Direction

🐎 Gallop
Lead foot steps forward
Trail foot closes up behind
Hips face forward throughout
Either leg can be the lead
Arms swing with the beat
VS
↔️ Slide
Lead foot steps sideways
Trail foot closes up beside it
Body turns to direction of travel
Either direction (left or right)
Arms swing with the beat
Both use the same step-together rhythm — master one and the other follows naturally.
PHE Canada, Fundamental Movement Skills Educator's Guide (2011)

Performance Indicators — PHE Canada

The 4 Teaching Cues

"Trail Leg"
Keep the trail leg behind the lead leg — it never crosses in front during the airborne phase
"Lead Leg"
Can gallop or slide using either leg as the lead — practice both sides equally
"Arms With Beat"
Rhythmical arm movement that matches the step-together pattern — arms help drive the rhythm
"Twist to Direction"
Hips, shoulders, and eyes all turn to face the direction of movement — not frozen facing forward
Stage 1 of 3

Initial Stage: Choppy Run

❌ Resembles Uneven Run
Looks more like an irregular running pattern than a true gallop. The rhythm is broken and unpredictable.
❌ Trail Leg Crosses Front
The trail leg crosses in front of the lead leg during the airborne phase and remains there at contact — the key error at this stage.
❌ No Rhythm
No clear beat or tempo. Each step looks different from the last. Arms are not yet helping.
❌ Choppy, Tense
Movement appears stiff and effortful. No fluidity between the step and the close.
Initial Stage K–1 typical
Stage 2 of 3

Elementary Stage: Stiff Back Leg

🔄 Slow-Moderate Tempo
Pattern starts to emerge but rhythm is choppy. Tempo is slow to moderate — improving from the chaotic initial stage.
🦵 Trail Leg Stiff
The trail leg stays behind the lead leg now (correct!) but remains stiff and extended rather than relaxed and bent.
↕️ Vertical Component Exaggerated
Too much up-and-down bounce. Energy is wasted vertically rather than directed forward or sideways.
↔️ Hips Oriented Sideways
Hips haven't fully rotated to face the direction of travel. The "Twist to Direction" cue is still needed here.
Elementary Stage Grades 1–3 typical
Stage 3 of 3 — The Goal

Mature Stage: Smooth & Rhythmical

✅ Smooth Rhythmical Pattern
Consistent, even tempo with a clear beat. Movement looks effortless and natural — no choppiness.
✅ Feet Remain Close to Ground
Flight phase is low and efficient. No wasted vertical energy. Weight transfers smoothly step to close.
✅ Hips Oriented Forward
Full hip rotation toward direction of travel. The "Twist to Direction" cue is fully executed — eyes, shoulders, hips all aligned.
✅ Arms Move With Beat
Arm swing is rhythmical and in sync with the step-together pattern. Arms actively drive the tempo.
Mature Stage Grades 2–5 goal

Difficulties to Watch For — PHE Canada

Common Errors & Fixes

If you see this… Then try this…
Inconsistent step-hop pattern Use colored tape to mark footsteps on the floor to help establish the pattern visually
Lack of rhythm in the movement Clap the rhythm for the child — or use a drum beat — so they can match their movement to the beat
Exaggerated, bouncy movements Encourage children to lift feet just off the ground — "quiet feet" or "sneak like a fox"
Slight loss of balance repeatedly Have the child slow down and exaggerate the "close" step — focus on control before speed
Landing flat-footed Ask children if they can make their gallop/slide as quiet as possible — forces forefoot landing
Assessment Protocol — TGMD-2

How We Assess

"Have the child gallop from one cone to the other for about 25 feet."
TGMD-2 Assessment Protocol
📋 What to observe
All 4 cues: trail leg position, lead leg alternation, arm rhythm, and hip/shoulder orientation toward direction of travel.
🔁 Both sides
Test galloping with the right leg leading AND the left leg leading. Both sides should be assessed separately.
Checklist
Trail leg stays behind lead leg
Either leg can be the lead
Arms move rhythmically with beat
Hips, shoulders, eyes twist to direction

Vocabulary — PHE Canada Literacy Component

Key Vocabulary Words

gallop
The primary skill — forward asymmetrical step-together movement
slide
The lateral version — same pattern moving sideways
forward
Direction of travel for the gallop
sideways
Direction of travel for the slide
open / close
The two parts of the step-together pattern — step open, then close
lead / follow
Lead foot goes first; trail (follow) foot closes behind it
Use these words consistently in class so students can understand and verbalize their movement. Language and movement develop together.
PHE Canada, Fundamental Movement Skills (2011)
Summary

Key Takeaways

🔢
3 Stages
Choppy Run → Stiff Back Leg → Smooth Rhythmical. The trail leg position is the clearest indicator of which stage a student is at.
📣
4 Cues
"Trail Leg" · "Lead Leg" · "Arms With Beat" · "Twist to Direction" — short, visual, actionable.
↔️
Same Skill, Two Planes
Gallop = forward. Slide = sideways. Same mechanics. Teach both together and reinforce the connection.
🔧
5 Common Errors
Trail leg crosses, no rhythm, too bouncy, loss of balance, flat feet. Each has a targeted floor-tape or drum-beat fix.
📏
Assessment — TGMD-2: Gallop 25 feet, both lead legs
Observe all 4 performance indicators per trial. Test the right leg leading AND the left leg leading — both sides matter equally.
